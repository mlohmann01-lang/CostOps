## Cross-playbook assumptions

1. The latest snapshot is authoritative, and playbooks fail if the requested snapshot is not the latest snapshot.
2. Evidence strings such as `user:<id>`, `license:<part>`, and `lastSignIn:<date>` are sufficient for explanation, but they do not currently include full source records or extraction timestamps per field.
3. Savings are always monthly savings multiplied by 12 for annual savings.
4. Default pricing is static and estimated unless environment overrides exist.
5. Candidate readiness starts as `APPROVAL_REQUIRED` unless candidate blockers exist, then the opportunity provider can block further based on global trust.
6. Current trust requirements are generic strings unless a playbook supplies custom requirements; none of the seven playbooks provide playbook-specific trust requirements.

## Inventory audit conclusion

The playbooks are useful for read-only discovery and prioritization, but most encode economic and safety assumptions that are not strong enough for live license removal. The strongest playbooks are those with explicit blockers or simple license-capacity evidence. The riskiest playbooks are those that infer entitlement overlap or user intent from SKU-name and usage heuristics.
